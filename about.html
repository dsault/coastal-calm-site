Our Story

Long before it became a sanctuary by the sea, this stretch of coastline in Mercedes, Poro had already witnessed generations of life, labor, and secrecy.

The Pearl Farm Era

Early pearl farming rafts on calm coastal waters — techniques brought from Japan in the early 20th century transformed places like this cove into quiet centers of cultivation.

In its earliest modern chapter, this land served as a working pearl farm. Drawing on techniques pioneered in Japan by Kokichi Mikimoto in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, the calm, protected waters of the Camotes became home to oyster cultivation.

Wooden rafts dotted the cove like quiet sentinels. Beneath the surface, thousands of oysters hung in mesh bags, each one carefully tended by workers who understood the slow, patient rhythm of the sea. The process was both art and science: inserting a small nucleus into the oyster, then waiting years for nature to coat it in layers of luminous nacre.

The mornings often began with the soft sound of water lapping against the rafts. Divers moved between the lines, checking the health of the oysters and protecting them from predators. At dusk, the same waters that nurtured the pearls reflected the sky in deep oranges and purples. For years, this cove quietly produced treasures that traveled far beyond the islands — small, glowing orbs born from the meeting of human care and the ocean’s mystery.

The legacy of those floating farms still lingers in local memory — a time when the sea here was not just a source of fish and livelihood, but a place where beauty was deliberately coaxed from the depths.

World War II and the Japanese Occupation

Coastal caves like these on Poro Island offered shelter to local families during the Japanese occupation of the Camotes in World War II.

During the Japanese occupation of the Philippines in World War II, the Camotes Islands fell under military control. Life for local residents became one of fear and uncertainty. Japanese forces maintained a presence across Poro Island, and stories of patrols, restrictions, and sudden violence were common.

Many families sought safety in the island’s natural caves — including those found along this very coastline. These shelters became temporary sanctuaries where people hid from patrols and waited for safer times. Some of the caves on and near the property still carry these stories in local memory. The fighting that eventually came to Poro Island in early 1945 was part of the final Allied push to liberate the Camotes.

Whispers of the Past

Decades after the war, local stories spoke of restricted areas and hidden rooms discovered in older structures on the land — tales that still linger in oral history.

After the war, as the islands slowly returned to a quieter rhythm, the property continued to change hands. With each transition, new layers of mystery were added. Over the decades, local oral history has preserved stories of a more enigmatic past — rumors of forbidden access, areas that were kept strictly off-limits, and the discovery of hidden rooms in older structures on the land.

These spaces reportedly had no clear official purpose. Elders still speak in hushed tones of sudden restrictions on movement and of things found that were never fully explained. While much of this remains in the realm of rumor and local legend, these tales have become part of the property’s enduring character, adding a quiet sense of mystery to its long coastal history.

A New Chapter

Today, we are writing a new chapter. D&D Coastal Calm is being thoughtfully redeveloped as a sustainable, eco-friendly boutique resort. Rather than erasing what came before, we are working in harmony with the land and sea. Solar power, responsible water systems, and low-impact design now guide our approach.

What was once a pearl farm, a wartime refuge, and later a place wrapped in local mystery and rumor, is now becoming a sanctuary — a peaceful destination where guests can slow down, connect with nature, and experience the quiet beauty of the Camotes as it was always meant to be experienced.

This land has held many stories.
We are honored to help shape the next one.
